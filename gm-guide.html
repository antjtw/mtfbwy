GM Guide

As the Game Master, you are not the adversary nor the author of a fixed tale — you are the living pulse of the world. These principles and tools help you run with confidence, clarity, and creativity.

GM Principles

Make the World Feel Real

Describe with all the senses. Show how factions scheme, landscapes shift, and rumours spread. Treat every part of the setting as though it lives and breathes — even beyond the players' sight.

Pitfall: Don't drown players in description. Give them enough to act, then let their questions guide the detail.

Play to Find Out What Happens

Don't script outcomes. Instead, set the stage, place moving parts in motion, and let the story emerge through the players' actions and the dice.

Pitfall: Railroading — forcing players back onto a predetermined path — robs their choices of meaning.

Frame, Don't Fumble

Start scenes in the middle of action or decision, not the slow lead-up. Keep momentum alive by asking, "What do you do?"

Pitfall: Letting play bog down in long travel logs or empty errands. Skip to the good stuff.

Adjudicate with Fairness

You are referee as much as narrator. Apply rules and consequences consistently, and when uncertain, make a ruling that keeps the game moving.

Pitfall: Playing favourites. Everyone at the table deserves the same chance to shine.

Share the Focus

Every character deserves moments to shine. Rotate attention, highlight different strengths, and ensure no one feels sidelined.

Pitfall: Letting louder players dominate while quiet ones fade into the background.

Let Choices Matter

Player decisions should echo through the world — altering relationships, tilting balances of power, leaving marks on the galaxy.

Pitfall: Undoing consequences just to protect your plans.

Say Yes, or Let the Dice Decide

If an idea fits the fiction, allow it. If there's uncertainty, call for a roll. Never stall creativity in the name of rigidity.

Pitfall: Shutting down clever ideas simply because they weren't anticipated.

Balance Challenge with Opportunity

Test the characters with danger, intrigue, or moral choices — but always pair hardship with the possibility of triumph or discovery.

Pitfall: Relentless punishment. Without hope or moments of victory, tension collapses into frustration.

Keep the Tone True

The galaxy reflects its genre. Carry mood through descriptions, NPC reactions, and consequences.

Pitfall: Jarring tone shifts that pull players out of the story (unless intentional).

Embrace Surprise, Even Your Own

Allow yourself to be surprised by the dice, by the table, and by where the story wants to go.

Pitfall: Treating the game like a performance where only you know the ending. The fun lies in discovering it together.

Rolling Dice

The GM does not use Dyad Dice. Instead, they roll a single d20, referred to as the GM's Die.

Adversary Attack Rolls

  1. Roll the GM's Die (d20) and add the adversary's attack bonus.
  2. If the total equals or exceeds the target's Evasion, the attack hits. Otherwise, it misses.
  3. On a hit, roll the attack's damage dice and apply the result.

Natural 20

A natural 20 always hits, striking with critical extra force. Roll damage normally, then add the highest possible number from the damage dice rolled. Flat damage modifiers are doubled.

Example: An attack dealing 3d6+2 would instead deal 18 + 3d6 + 2.

Adversary Advantage & Disadvantage

If an adversary has Advantage on a GM Die roll, roll two d20s and keep the higher. If they have Disadvantage, roll two d20s and keep the lower.

GM Moves

Your moves shape the story in response to the players' actions. GM moves happen during GM turns — a GM turn begins when the Focus shifts to you, and ends when you hand it back to the players.

When to Make a GM Move

Make a GM move when players:

  • Roll with Despair
  • Fail an attack, skill check, or saving roll
  • Take an action with unavoidable consequences
  • Hand you a golden opportunity (an opening that demands a response)
  • Look to you to decide what happens next

Choosing GM Moves by Roll Result

Critical Success
The player describes their success, then you grant them an additional opportunity and advantage.
Success with Balance
The player describes their success, and you show how the Galaxy opens a new opportunity in response.
Success with Despair
The player succeeds, but at a cost. They describe the success, then you take a Despair and make a GM move to introduce a minor consequence.
Failure with Balance
The PC doesn't get what they want, but the setback is not catastrophic. Describe the failure, then introduce a minor consequence.
Failure with Despair
Things go badly — and dangerously. Describe how it unravels, then introduce a major consequence.

Yes/But/No Framework

Success with Balance: Yes, and…

You succeed cleanly, and the moment creates a new opportunity.

Success with Despair: Yes, but…

You succeed, but there's a consequence. The GM gains a Despair.

Failure with Balance: No, but…

Things don't go as planned, but you gain a new opportunity.

Failure with Despair: No, and…

You fail, and it gets worse. The GM gains a Despair.

Move Severity

Soft moves give players information and a chance to react — tension, foreshadowing. Hard moves escalate conflict, inflict consequences, or close off options. Use softer moves on rolls with Balance, and harsher moves on rolls with Despair.

Example GM Moves

  • Show how the world reacts
  • Ask a question and build on the answer
  • Make an NPC act in line with their motive
  • Drive a PC to act by pressing their goals
  • Signal an imminent off-screen threat
  • Reveal an unwelcome truth or unexpected danger
  • Force the group to split up
  • Have a PC expend a resource
  • Make a move the characters don't see
  • Show collateral damage
  • Clear an adversary's condition
  • Shift the environment
  • Focus on an adversary
  • Capture someone or something important
  • Use a PC's backstory against them
  • Take away an opportunity permanently

Using Despair

Despair is the GM's narrative fuel — used to escalate tension and shift momentum. You start a campaign with 1 Despair per adventurer. You gain Despair whenever a PC rolls with Despair, when the PCs rest, or when an ability instructs you to. Despair carries over between sessions.

Spending Despair

  • Interrupt the players and take the Focus with an Adversary, allowing them to take a regular turn
  • Make an additional GM move
  • Activate an Adversary's Despair Feature
  • Trigger an environmental Despair Feature

Spending Tactics

  • Spending Fast: Drop Despair before players can react, creating surprise
  • Spending Often: Use Despair whenever Focus returns to you, keeping pressure constant
  • Spending Big: Spend multiple Despair in quick succession to overwhelm the party

Difficulty Benchmarks

  • Attack rolls vs adversaries: Difficulty equals the adversary's Evasion
  • Other action rolls vs adversaries: Difficulty equals the adversary's Difficulty score plus any relevant Trait modifier
  • Unspecified rolls: When no set Difficulty exists, assign one based on the situation

A quick rule of thumb: 12 + relevant modifier is your baseline for any unspecified Difficulty.

Cinematic Guidance

When uncertain, choose drama over precision. The exact number matters less than the moment it creates. Let the dice support the story, not slow it down.

Countdowns

Countdowns represent a ticking clock — events running out before something happens. A countdown starts at a set value and decreases as it advances. When it reaches 0, its effect is triggered.

Standard Countdowns

A standard countdown decreases by 1 every time a player makes a Dyad Dice roll. If an adversary, ability, or environment feature refers to a "Countdown [n]," it means a standard countdown starting at n.

Dynamic Countdowns

Dynamic countdowns shift based on outcomes, moving up to 3 steps per roll.

Roll ResultProgress AdvancementConsequence Advancement
Failure with DespairNo advancementTick down 3
Failure with BalanceNo advancementTick down 2
Success with DespairTick down 1Tick down 1
Success with BalanceTick down 2No advancement
Critical SuccessTick down 3No advancement

Advanced Countdown Features

Randomised: Start at a hidden or random value
Loop: Reset to their starting value once triggered
Increasing: Each loop starts with +1 to its value
Decreasing: Each loop starts with −1 to its value
Linked: Progress and Consequence countdowns both advance on the same rolls
Long-Term: Advance only after rests instead of every roll

Concluding an Encounter

When combat ends — through defeat of a Leader, retreat or surrender of adversaries, or the GM declaring the threat resolved — the GM may spend 3 Despair to initiate a Strenuous Combat Saving Roll once the physical encounter concludes.

Each Hero must make a Resilience Saving Roll. The base Difficulty is 12. The GM may increase this by spending additional Despair (e.g., spending 5 total Despair sets the Difficulty to 14).

Roll ResultEffect
Critical SuccessThe Hero finds a second wind — all Willpower slots restored
Success with BalanceThe Hero comes away steady — restore 1 Willpower slot
Success with DespairThe Hero holds together — no penalty to Willpower
Failure with BalanceThe Hero overexerts themselves — mark off 1 Willpower slot
Failure with DespairThe Hero is drained — mark off all remaining Willpower slots

Running NPCs

NPCs bring the world to life through their goals, quirks, and methods. Treat them as dynamic characters rather than background props. Give them names, motivation, and a physical description — ideally on an NPC Card.

NPC Cards

Each NPC Card captures:

  • Goal — what they want
  • Method — how they achieve it (charm, lies, brute force, etc.)
  • Anchor — a quirk, phrase, or memorable detail
  • Attitude — Indifferent, Friendly, or Hostile
Example NPC Card: Gary Woodland
Bouncer for Global Bastards Ltd

Goal: Earn credits purely for personal gain

Method: Takes any job going, but puts in the bare minimum effort

Anchor: Picks his teeth with a claw while others talk, staring off as if half elsewhere

Attitude: Indifferent. Rarely listens. Detached, often agrees without caring.

A purple-scaled Tiss'Shar with jagged, predatory teeth and a ridge-like crest crowning his head. He sports a battered bright-blue Berghaus jacket that barely conceals his weapons beneath.

NPCs in Combat

Keep NPCs narrative unless mechanics matter. Most NPCs don't need hit points or willpower unless they interact mechanically. Focus on their role in the scene and reward PCs who leverage their help.

Example NPC Features

Hold Still

Choose: At encounter start, choose a favoured PC.
Trigger: That PC makes a melee attack near the NPC.
Effect: NPC restrains the enemy if their Difficulty meets the Adversary's. PC gains Advantage on the attack.

Sage Advice

Choose: At encounter start, select a pupil PC.
Trigger: Pupil is within Close range and fails a roll.
Effect: NPC provides advice; the PC rerolls with Advantage.

Blaster Volley

Trigger: NPC joins a firefight.
Effect: Start a countdown (Loop 3). On trigger, NPC fires a volley at a PC target: 2d8+3 NRG damage.

Professional Business Being

Trigger: NPC shares a full rest with the party.
Effect: Roll 1d4. On 1–2, the NPC vanishes — stealing 1d100×4 credits.

Awarding Players

Rewards keep the game world alive and give players a sense of progress. When awarding loot, credits, or benefits, think about what fits the story, the setting, and the players' motivations.

Make Rewards Meaningful

A battered blaster pulled from a swoop ganger's belt tells a story. An anonymous item dropped from nowhere breaks immersion. Place loot where it belongs and let it carry history.

Mix Material and Narrative

Credits, weapons, and upgrades are valuable, but so are allies, reputation, rare knowledge, and favours owed. Blending the two ensures every kind of player feels recognised.

Keep Credits Scarce but Relevant

Credits should grease the wheels, not erase tension. Pay the crew enough to keep them flying, but not enough to live easy.

Reward Choices, Not Just Encounters

The richest prizes come from player agency — bargaining for a better payout, pushing deeper into danger, or forging a bond with an NPC.

Adversary Types

Minions: Disposable. Pressure through numbers. Battle droids, gang footsoldiers.
Squads: Groups acting as a single coordinated unit. Stormtrooper fireteams.
Standards: Baseline faction representatives. The backbone of most encounters.
Enforcers: Tough frontline combatants. Trandoshan hunters, riot troopers.
Skirmishers: Mobile, opportunistic. Assassins, bounty hunters, scouts.
Marksmen: High-damage ranged. Snipers, heavy gunners, turret operators.
Specialists: Defined by a specific threat or toolset. Slicers, demolitionists, medics.
Commanders: Issue orders, reposition allies, call reinforcements. Imperial officers.
Adepts: Force, Magick, or Tech casters. Inquisitors, Nightsisters, combat slicers.
Support Units: Strengthen allies, disrupt Heroes. Shield bearers, morale officers.
Influencers: Non-combat tension through authority or manipulation. Crime bosses.
Nemeses: Challenge an entire party alone. Often have multiple phases. Sith rivals.

Stats at a Glance

Adversaries use the same trait array as Heroes (+2, +1, +1, +0, +0, −1), then may further increase traits by tier:

TierTrait IncreasesProficiency Bonus
Tier 0+2 to one trait, or +1 to two traits+2
Tier 1+4 to one trait, or spread+4
Tier 2+6 to one trait, or spread+6
Tier 3+8 to one trait, or spread+8

Adversaries do not have a Minor damage threshold. Any successful hit that deals 1 or more damage marks Hit Point slots, unless a feature states otherwise.

Adversary Actions

When an adversary is Focused, it may perform one of:

  • Two manoeuvres, or
  • One manoeuvre and one combat action, or
  • One manoeuvre and one ritual progression

Adversaries do not track Reaction Slots — they may react whenever a listed trigger occurs, provided it makes narrative sense.

Willpower for Adversaries

Recommended only for Commanders, Nemeses, or other key adversaries whose defeat should carry narrative weight. Willpower may fuel powers or features, and primarily delays death:

  • If Hit Point Slots are depleted but Willpower remains, the adversary gains the Dying condition
  • If Willpower is depleted while Hit Point Slots remain, the adversary falls Unconscious

Using Environments

Environments are not backdrops — they are active participants in a scene. A strong environment pressures Heroes even when no adversary is acting, and gives you tools to escalate tension without adding more enemies.

Environmental Features

Passives: Always in effect. Toxic fog, extreme heat, unstable ground reducing movement.
Reactions: Activate in response to specific actions. Alarms when doors are forced, collapsing walkways under heavy weapons fire.
Despair Features: High-impact events triggered by spending Despair. Explosions, cave-ins, system overloads.
Countdowns: Escalating threats that resolve when a countdown reaches zero. Reactor meltdown, flooding chambers, spreading fire.
🎯

If the environment begins driving decisions more than the fight itself, you are using it correctly.

Design Guidance

Most encounters shine when built with 2–3 different types, creating movement, pressure, and tactical texture. Minions and Squads establish scale. Standards and Skirmishers form the backbone. Nemeses define the encounter's narrative weight.
