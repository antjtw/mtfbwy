Reference & Appendix

Languages, companions, equipment, environment, travel, downtime, and the full rules for Kessel Sabacc. Everything you might need between sessions — or mid-adventure.

Languages

Galactic Basic
Core Rules

Known as Standard Galactic Basic, or Galactic Standard — the most prevalent language in the galaxy, with a number of different dialects and accents.

If you speak or understand Galactic Basic, you can read and understand the Aurebesh, High Galactic, and Outer Rim Basic scripts.

Binary
Core Rules

Also known as droidspeak or astromech — the language commonly spoken by droids. It consists of beeps and whistles strung together into what resembles sentences. Binary often needs to be translated for organics via computer systems, though some individuals can understand it natively.

Huttese
Core Rules

A language often spoken on Hutt-controlled worlds, originating from the Hutt species. Also a widespread trade language. Huttese shares structural similarities to Aqualish and Rodian, so a speaker may infer meaning from any of these languages.

Rodian
Core Rules

Also known as Rodese — a language spoken by members of the Rodian species. Shares structural similarities to Huttese and Aqualish, so a speaker may infer meaning from any of these languages.

Shyriiwook
Core Rules

The language of the Wookiees. It consists of growling and howls — difficult for anyone not a Wookiee to speak or learn. It has over one hundred and fifty words for wood, and many words for bloodshed. Those who understand Shyriiwook may also read Wookiee Script.

Twi'leki (Ryl)
Core Rules

A language spoken by Twi'leks from Ryloth. It combines verbal sounds and an elaborate sign language using the Twi'lek's lekku. That sign language allows the Twi'leks to communicate complete concepts without speaking a single word. Species without head-tails may only be able to commune in verbal Twi'leki.

Low Core Basic
Galactic Antiquities

A form of Basic that uses a different script entirely, and cannot be interpreted by speakers of Galactic Basic.

Companions

Heroes often journey with loyal droids, cunning creatures, or enigmatic entities. Companions support the Hero without overshadowing them. They scout, slice, heal, distract, bolster, or simply add heart and narrative weight.

Companions never seize the Focus unless a feature explicitly allows it. They act as reactive, narrative-first, mechanically-light allies.

Gaining a Companion

A companion is gained by spending two Level-Up Options during character progression.

Rules for Ownership

  • A Hero may only have one active companion at a time
  • Companions gain +1 and +2 to any two Trait Modifiers, chosen when acquired
  • A companion's Tier is based on the Hero's current level

Defences

  • Evasion = Companion's Agility Modifier + 12
  • Major Damage Threshold = Tier value + Companion's Resilience Modifier
  • Companions do not benefit from Armour Slots

Damage & Healing

When reduced to 0 Hit Point slots, a companion drops Unconscious and cannot be revived until the next Short or Full Rest. Unlike Heroes, once dropped to 0 Hit Points during an encounter, they cannot be revived during that encounter.

While conscious, companions can be healed by normal means. Once unconscious, use the Tend to Wounds downtime move.

Upgrading a Companion

Once a Hero advances to the next tier, their companion becomes eligible to upgrade — but this doesn't happen automatically. The Hero must use the Working on a Project Downtime Move.

Death or Destruction

When damage would knock a Companion unconscious, the GM may instead choose to spend 8 Despair to kill or destroy the Companion outright.

📖

The death or destruction of a Companion should serve the story and heighten drama — not be used as an opportunistic punishment. Treat Companion loss as a meaningful narrative beat, not a routine consequence.

Equipment Categories

Equipment in MTFBYW spans weapons, tools, devices, and everyday gear used across the galaxy. Full item lists are available in the MTFBYW Equipment Spreadsheet.

Weapons

All ranged and melee weapons — from vibroweapons and lightsabers to blasters and exotic martial devices. Each weapon lists its type, damage, traits, range, and burden.

Armour

Provides Armour Slots, special resistances, or unique effects. The armour type determines weight and movement impact.

Shields

Provide defensive benefits and may grant Armour Slots or reactions. Most shields occupy a hand and often have a Burden value.

Adventuring Gear

Rations, glowrods, climbing gear, breathing masks, survival kits, restraints, tents, comms equipment, and more.

Tools

Medpacs, slicing kits, repair tools, scanners, field kits, databanks, and alchemical sets. Allow adventurers to perform specialised tasks or unlock gameplay options.

Explosives & Ordnance

Grenades, breaching charges, mines, and other single-use destructive tools. Typically consume inventory slots.

Powers, Foci & Mystical Devices

Holocrons, Jedi tomes, relics, amulets, charms, ritual components, and sacred items. Many are held or worn.

Storage & Containers

Backpacks, bandoliers, utility belts, satchels, pouches, cybernetic compartments, and specialised gear cases. Often modify the number of inventory slots available.

Inventory & Burden

Inventory Slots

All characters begin with 4 inventory slots — representing basic belts, pockets, and person-carried storage. Backpacks, bandoliers, utility belts, and species traits may increase this.

The following never count against your inventory capacity: power cells, ammunition, casting components, and very small personal items (at the GM's discretion).

If you exceed your slot limit, you must expend 1 Willpower for each slot you exceed your capacity. This cost is paid when you first exceed capacity, and again each time you further increase your overburdened total.

Burden

Burden determines how many hands are required to use a weapon, item, device, or power. If you do not have enough free hands, you cannot perform that action.

BurdenRules
One-HandedRequires one free hand. You may hold another one-handed item in the off-hand, or cast powers requiring a free hand.
Two-HandedRequires two free hands. You cannot hold or carry any other hand-occupying item. You cannot cast one-handed powers simultaneously.
VersatileCan be used with one or two hands. When used two-handed, the item gains the bonus listed in its description.
Dual-Handed PowersSome Force or Magick abilities require two free hands to channel properly.

Use the Free Manoeuvre: Prepare Item to put items away, draw items, drop items, or swap items between hands.

Environment

Burning

A character or object that is Burning takes 1d4 FIR damage when the Burning effect advances, using a Dynamic Countdown (4). As a manoeuvre, a creature can attempt to extinguish flames on itself by dropping prone and rolling on the ground.

Dehydration

Organic Heroes require water each day. If a creature drinks less than half its required water in a day, it marks off 1 Willpower slot at the end of that day. Willpower lost to dehydration cannot be restored until the creature drinks its full daily requirement.

Falling

A creature that falls takes 1d6 PHY damage for every 3 metres fallen, to a maximum of 20d6. On landing, the creature gains the Prone condition unless it takes no damage.

If a creature falls into water, it may use a Trigger move to make a Difficulty 18 Strength (Athletics) or Agility (Coordination) saving roll to enter the surface cleanly. On a success, damage from the fall is halved.

Hostile Environments

Extreme Heat (40°C+)

Each hour, organics make a Resilience Saving Roll. Difficulty starts at 6, increasing by +2 each consecutive hour. On a failure, mark off 1 Willpower slot.

Extreme Cold (−20°C or below)

Each hour, organics and droids make a Difficulty 12 Resilience Saving Roll. On a failure, mark off 1 Willpower slot.

Crushing Pressure

Each hour, exposed entities make a Difficulty 12 Resilience Saving Roll. On a failure, mark off 1 Willpower slot. Prolonged exposure may impose Weakened.

Vacuum of Space

A Dynamic Countdown (4) begins immediately. When the countdown advances, make a Difficulty 18 Resilience Saving Roll. Failure: mark 6 Willpower slots and take Major CLD damage. Success: mark 3 Willpower slots and take Minor CLD damage.

Suffocation

An organic can hold its breath for a number of minutes equal to 1 + its Resilience modifier (minimum 30 seconds). Once they run out of breath, they mark off 1 Willpower slot following a Dynamic Countdown (4). While suffocating, the creature cannot recover Willpower by any means. When they can breathe again, all Willpower lost due to suffocation is immediately restored.

Marching Order

While travelling, the group should establish a marching order — who is at the front, centre, and rear. Marching order helps the GM determine which Heroes are affected by traps or ambushes, who notices hidden threats first, and who is closest to danger when combat breaks out.

Social Interaction

Not every conflict is solved with blasters and blades. Social interaction covers persuasion, deception, intimidation, bargaining, and negotiation, and plays out through roleplay, skill checks, or both.

Downtime

When the party rests, each adventurer can make downtime moves. These are moments for recovery and for quiet personal scenes that shape the story between battles.

🌑
Downtime Consequences

The world doesn't stop when you rest. On a short rest, the GM gains 1d4 Despair. On a full rest, the GM gains Despair equal to 1d4 + the number of PCs. Be cautious about how many rests your party takes.

A short rest is about an hour. Each player may choose two moves from the list below.

Tend to Wounds: Clear 1d4 Hit Point slots. You may do this on an ally. No power or item required.
Repair Armour: Clear 1d4 Armour Slots. You may do this on an ally's armour. No power or item required.
Restore Willpower: Clear 1d4 Willpower slots. No power or item required.
Strategise: Put together a plan of action. If you stick to your plan, you gain Advantage on rolls that follow your strategy.
Repair: Spend time repairing an item, weapon, or piece of technology (requires the specific tool). Simple repairs resolve with a single Dyad Dice roll. Complex repairs use a Target Score.

A full rest is when characters find improvised shelter, relax for a few hours, and get some sleep. Each player may choose two moves.

Tend to All Wounds: Remove all marked Hit Points. You may do this on an ally.
Repair All Armour: Clear all Armour Slots. You may do this on an ally.
Recover All Willpower: Clear all Willpower slots.
Strategise: As per short rest.
Repair: As per short rest. Full rest allows full repair of most items.
Work on a Project: Establish or continue work on a long-term project.

The Working on a Project downtime move allows Heroes to undertake tasks requiring sustained effort over multiple full rests. The player and GM agree on the nature of the project and assign a Target Score.

Kessel Sabacc

Kessel Sabacc is a fast-paced gambling game played across cantinas and starships throughout the galaxy. It combines chance, bluffing, and strategy as players try to manage their hands while risking their chips.

Goal: Be the last player with chips remaining. Best played with 2–4 players.

Components

Two decks of 22 cards each:

  • 3× value cards each of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 (18 cards total)
  • 3× Impostor cards
  • 1× Sylop card

You can also play using a single d8 for each deck (rolling 1–6 represents the value cards). Each player begins with 48 chips.

Special Cards

Sylop

Copies the value of the other card in your hand during Resolution. Two Sylops together form Pure Sabacc — the unbeatable hand.

Impostor

During Resolution, roll 2d6 and choose one result to determine its value.

Round Structure

Each round consists of: Setup → Three Turns → Resolution.

Early End Rule: If all players choose to Stand before three turns are complete, proceed immediately to Resolution.

On Your Turn, Choose One:

Stand

Take no action and keep your current hand.

Draw

Spend 1 chip from your Stock into your Pot. Draw one card or roll a d8 from either the Sand or Blood deck (or from the top of a discard pile). You may accept or refuse the draw — if you refuse, place the drawn card on the discard pile. You must always end your turn with one Sand card and one Blood card.

Resolution

  1. Resolve Special Card Effects (in this exact order)
    1. Double Sylops: Both count as value 0 (Pure Sabacc)
    2. Blood Impostors: Roll 2d6, choose one result
    3. Sand Impostors: Roll 2d6, choose one result
    4. Single Sylop: Copies the value of your other card
  2. Calculate Hand Value

    Hand Value = absolute difference between Sand and Blood card values. A Hand Value of 0 = Sabacc.

  3. Rank Players

    Sabacc players (value = 0): compare highest single card value — lowest wins. Non-Sabacc players: rank by Hand Value (lowest first). Ties broken by total sum of both cards (lowest wins).

  4. Pay Chips
    • Winner (Rank 1): Retrieve all chips from your Pot back to your Stock
    • Sabacc but not winner: Pay 1 chip to the Pot
    • Non-Sabacc players: Pay chips equal to their Hand Value to the Pot
  5. Eliminate Players

    Players with 0 Stock are eliminated. If only one player remains, they win the game.

Starship Attributes

  • Evasion: 12 + Manoeuvrability modifier + any other bonuses
  • Shield Slots: Rather than Armour Slots, starships have Shield Slots. When a starship takes damage, Shield Slots are expended first.
  • Hull Slots: Once all Shield Slots are expended, damage rolls over onto Hull Slots. When all Hull Slots are expended, the starship is destroyed.

Core Systems

Power Core: Provides energy. Without it, life support, sublight engines, and weapons cannot function.
Life Support: Makes the interior capable of supporting life. Without it, crew must wear environment suits.
Sublight Engines: Allow the ship to travel through space below the speed of light.
Navigation Computer: Makes calculations for hyperspace navigation. Also known as an astrogation computer or navicomp.
Hyperdrive: Allows the ship to reach lightspeed and traverse hyperspace.

Modifications & Purchase

Most modifications require access to a shipyard, skilled mechanics, and sufficient credits — typically taking the ship out of active service for a meaningful stretch of time. Some minor upgrades may be performed in the field.

Purchasing a starship is a major investment. Costs vary widely based on size, condition, age, and existing modifications. Heroes may attempt to barter or negotiate a better price. Buying a ship is as much about relationships and timing as it is about money.
